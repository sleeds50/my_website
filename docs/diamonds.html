Diamonds

Stuart Leeds

19/03/2022

Introduction

Are diamonds really a girls best friend? Are they even forever? They are certainly expensive; and they last a long time. Diamonds are the hardest known natural material and are developed deep underground over millennia of immense pressure and temperature. See the Wikipedia entry for general information about diamonds.

The purpose of this short article is to analyse a dataset of diamond information using various R packages. We are interested in the descriptive statistics of the dataset; the correlation between price and the main variables; and whether the relationship between carat and the price of diamonds is mediated by cut, color and clarity.

Data

The diamonds data are found in ggplot2 (Wickham et al., 2023) and consist of almost 54,000 round-cut diamonds with the following variables:

  1. Carat (weight of the diamond): 0.2 to 5.01ct.

  2. Cut (quality of the cut): Fair, Good, Very Good, Premium, Ideal.

  3. Color: [Best] D, E, F, G, H, I, J [Worst].

  4. Clarity (how clear the diamond is): [Worst] I1, SI2, SI1, VS2, VS1, VVS2, VVS1, IF [Best].

  5. Depth (total depth percentage): 43 to 79%, calculated with either \[\frac{z}{mean(x, y)}\times 100 \quad \text{or}\quad \frac{2 \times z}{(x + y)}\times 100\]

  6. Table (width of top of diamond relative to widest point [percentage of diameter]): 43 to 95%.

  7. Price (US$): 326 to 18823.

    The variables below are used to calculate Depth (Item 5), and are not used in any analysis in this article:
    x (length): 0 to 10.74mm.
    y (width): 0 to 58.9mm.
    z (depth): 0 to 31.8mm.

Results

Correlation

Correlational analysis shows the relationships between variables whether significant, or otherwise (see Figure 2 below). As expected all the main variables in the diamond data set are significantly correlated with price. Of the significant positive relationships, carat is the strongest (\(r=\) \(0.92\), \(p< .001\)), with weak correlations with price found for color (\(r=\) \(0.17\), \(p< .001\)). The remaining variables have negative, weak, yet significant correlations with price: clarity (\(r=\) \(-0.15\), \(p< .001\)) and cut (\(r=\) \(-0.05\), \(p< .001\)).

Figure 2. Correlation Plot of Diamonds Data with the Main Variables


The variance of each correlation with price can be explained with \(r^2\) (see Figure 3 below). For example, \(85\%\) of the variance in the price of diamonds can be explained by carat (\(r^2=\) \(0.85\)). The same principle applies to the other correlations: color (\(r^2=\) \(0.03\) explains \(3\%\) of the variance in price); clarity (\(r^2=\) \(0.02\); \(2\%\)); and cut (\(r^2=\) \(0.003\)); \(0.3\%\)).
